Well, in the simplest terms, I've got the three great virtues most required of programmers; laziness, impatience, and hubris. Not to mention the five mannerisms of programmers; I quote Monty Python, I love cats, I play guitar (classical), I read Heinlein and I'm a punster. Put all that together and you've got one hell of a coder on your hands.

I can sit and hack for up to five hours (that's without getting up for a drink or to piss). If you include those times, I've pulled up to three days (without sleep). That's just for fun, too. If I was getting paid, I may even put out some effort and get more done. Why don't you offer me some money for a creative project and we'll see ;)

If I was to pick a language to hack, it would be PERL. Of course, I like a good OO language, like Java. I don't think C is bad, but if you ask me to write C++, you can expect C code with C++ commenting. I don't think there's any reason for C++ to exist. VB can just give up now. I've had to use it, but it's proprietary and slow, so I avoid it whenever possible. If you want my opinion on any other languages (which I'm sure you do), feel free to ask.

You want me to write code for what!? I'm a UN*X boy. It'd take a pack of wild dogs to drag me away from a nice Linux box. I'll deal with Windows and Macs, but don't expect me to enjoy it. You want PERL code for a Win32 machine? Well, I'll write the UN*X code and make whatever changes I have to, but don't expect it to be "developed for Windows". There's no point in it. Developing for UN*X and porting to Windows will get you a better application nine out of ten times anyway. Especially if you want to use Microsoft's compilers. Yuck! Talk about bad code.

Now you've got the low-down on me as a programmer. I really quite good at it. I may have my high horse and moral stances, but once you get past it, I'll toss code at you like nothing else. Once I get out of college (only eight or nine more years ;), I'll be on the open market, so get ready to start bidding.

hubris
Excessive pride, the sort of thing Zeus zaps you for. Also the quality that makes you write (and maintain) programs that other people won't want to say bad things about. Hence, the third great virtue of a programmer. From "Programming Perl" by Larry Wall et. al.
impatience
The anger you feel when the computer is being lazy. This makes you write programs that don't just react to your needs, but actually anticipate them. Or at least that pretend to. Hence the second great virtue of a programmer. From "Programming Perl" by Larry Wall et. al.
laziness
The quality that makes you go to great effort to reduce overall energy expenditure. It makes you write labor-saving programs that other people will find useful, and document what you wrote so you don't have to answer so many questions about it. Hence, the first great virtue of a programmer. From "Programming Perl" by Larry Wall et. al.
